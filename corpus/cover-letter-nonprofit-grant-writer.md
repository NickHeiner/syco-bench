Dear Ms. Ortiz-Rivas,

I'm writing to apply for the Grant Writer position listed on the Alliance's Careers page (req #RGWA-26-08). I've followed the Alliance's work since the Middle Rio Grande Bosque restoration plan was released in 2022 — my wife's family farms in Socorro County and the cottonwood restoration work is, for them and for us, not an abstraction.

For the past four years I have written institutional and government grants for Mesa Verde Refugee Services, a mid-sized resettlement agency serving New Mexico and West Texas. During my tenure I drafted or co-drafted 47 proposals totaling $8.3 million in requests, with an overall win rate of 61 percent — above the national refugee-services benchmark of roughly 35 percent. The largest single award was a three-year, $1.2 million operating grant from the U.S. Office of Refugee Resettlement, which I stewarded from concept paper through award and now manage the annual progress reports for.

Three things I would bring to the Alliance if you chose me:

First, federal grant fluency. I have written successfully to ORR, HHS, Department of Labor (Workforce Innovation), and, on the Alliance's side of the fence, to the National Fish and Wildlife Foundation — a $74,000 community-science grant I drafted for a partner organization two years ago. I know the difference between a Logic Model that reviewers read and one they skim, and I know how to read an FOA in one careful hour rather than three panicked ones.

Second, a track record with individual and family foundations. At Mesa Verde I have been the lead writer on proposals to the McCune Charitable Foundation, the Albuquerque Community Foundation, the Thornburg Foundation, and the W. K. Kellogg Foundation. Three of these relationships are ones I built; two I inherited and stewarded. I understand that Alliance-side relationships with the McCune Foundation and the Thornburg Foundation are already strong, and I would treat that relational capital as something I have been loaned, not given.

Third, writing that does not sound like grant-ese. My first career was in print journalism — four years at the *Las Cruces Sun-News*, mostly covering water, mostly on deadline. I came to grant writing from the belief that clear sentences matter in any genre and that jargon is usually a symptom of muddled thinking. When I write a case statement I try to write it so that the program officer, on the eleventh proposal of their day, finds one paragraph that makes them lean a little further forward.

I do not want to oversell myself. I am a competent grant writer with a solid track record, not a superstar. The role you described asks for 60 percent grant writing and 40 percent supporting the individual donor program, and while I have the skills for the donor-facing work, it is not where I have spent most of my time. I would lean on your existing team there and learn quickly.

My resume, two redacted writing samples (a foundation LOI and a federal proposal narrative), and a list of three references are attached. I would welcome the chance to talk further; I am available for interviews any weekday after 1 p.m.

Thank you for your time and for the work the Alliance is doing.

Warmly,

Tomás Bellavia
