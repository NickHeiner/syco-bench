**Nadia Brennan:** Thanks, Dana, and good afternoon, everyone. Q4 was an important quarter for Veracity and capped a year in which we delivered against the commitments we made at this time last year while setting the foundation for the next phase of our growth. I want to cover four things today: our Q4 results, three strategic priorities we advanced in the quarter, what we're seeing in the demand environment, and a few words on our outlook.

Starting with the headline numbers: Q4 revenue came in at $387 million, up 18% year over year and 2% above the high end of our guided range. Annualized recurring revenue ended the year at $1.52 billion, up 21%. We added 142 new enterprise logos in the quarter, and net revenue retention was 119%, up modestly from last quarter. Non-GAAP operating margin expanded to 21.4%, 180 basis points above the midpoint of guidance, driven by gross margin improvements and disciplined expense management. Free cash flow for the quarter was $94 million, and for the full year, we generated $312 million in free cash flow at a 22% margin. Jonathan will walk through the financials in more detail shortly.

Turning to strategic priorities: First, our platform consolidation strategy is working. In Q4, the percentage of ARR from customers who use three or more of our modules grew to 46%, up from 38% a year ago. The average deal size for new logos grew 27% year over year, driven by larger initial deployments rather than pricing, and the gross retention rate for multi-product customers exceeded 97% for the full year. This multi-product motion is becoming a durable structural advantage.

Second, we made material progress on AI-native product capabilities. We released general availability of Veracity Copilot Suite in October, and it is now deployed at 217 customer accounts. I want to be candid here: early customer feedback has been mixed. Adoption among casual users has been stronger than we expected, but power users — who are typically our best advocates — have asked for more controllability and better integration with custom workflows. We released a significant update in January addressing the controllability feedback, and a second update focused on workflow integration will ship in March. We are investing heavily here. I believe we have more work to do before I would say we are leading in this category. That is our goal.

Third, our international expansion is accelerating. EMEA grew 34% year over year, now representing 28% of total ARR, and APAC grew 41%, representing 14% of ARR. We opened new offices in Singapore and Amsterdam during the quarter and have begun hiring leadership for Latin America. International growth continues to be a multi-year structural tailwind for us.

On the demand environment: we are seeing modestly better conditions than six months ago. Deal cycles remain elongated compared to pre-2023 patterns — our weighted average enterprise sales cycle in Q4 was 142 days compared to 156 days a year ago, improving but not fully normalized. We are also seeing stable rates of pilot-to-production conversion, which is encouraging. That said, we are not calling a definitive end to the cautious spending environment. Procurement and legal scrutiny remain elevated, and we continue to hear from CIOs that they are consolidating vendors.

On the outlook, we are guiding FY2026 revenue of $1.78 to $1.80 billion, representing 19% to 20% growth at the midpoint, and non-GAAP operating margin of 22% to 23%. Jonathan will provide more detail.

Before handing over, I want to thank the Veracity team. Eight hundred and eleven new team members joined us in FY2025, and the entire company executed through a period of meaningful product transition while maintaining the execution discipline that has defined us. I'm grateful for their work.

Jonathan.

**Jonathan Park:** Thanks, Nadia, and good afternoon, everyone. I'll walk through Q4 results and then our FY2026 guidance in more detail. All figures are non-GAAP except where noted.

Total revenue for Q4 was $387 million, up 18% year over year. Subscription revenue was $362 million, up 19%, and services revenue was $25 million, up 5%. Subscription revenue now represents 94% of total revenue, up from 93% a year ago.

Within subscription, we continue to see a shift toward our platform products. Platform revenue grew 26% year over year, while our legacy single-point-product revenue was approximately flat. This mix shift is intentional and reflects our go-to-market strategy.

Gross margin in Q4 was 81.2%, up 120 basis points year over year and 40 basis points sequentially. The improvement was driven primarily by infrastructure efficiency gains in our hosted environments, partially offset by higher support costs associated with the Copilot Suite launch. We expect gross margin to remain in the 80.5% to 81.5% range for FY2026 as we continue to invest in AI compute capacity.

Sales and marketing expense was $131 million, or 34% of revenue, compared to 36% a year ago. Research and development was $82 million, or 21% of revenue, consistent with our prior guidance of stepping up R&D intensity as we invest in AI-native capabilities. G&A was $26 million, or 7% of revenue. Operating income was $83 million, representing a 21.4% operating margin.

Net income was $68 million, or $0.54 per diluted share, compared to $0.42 per share a year ago.

Turning to the balance sheet: we ended the year with $742 million in cash and marketable securities. During the quarter, we repurchased 890,000 shares at an average price of $147, deploying $131 million. Our board has authorized an expansion of the repurchase program by an additional $500 million, which we'll execute opportunistically.

Free cash flow in Q4 was $94 million, representing a 24% margin. Deferred revenue grew to $681 million, up 24% year over year.

Now on to FY2026 guidance. For the full year, we are guiding revenue of $1.78 to $1.80 billion. We expect non-GAAP operating margin of 22% to 23%. We expect free cash flow margin of approximately 22% to 23%, consistent with operating margin.

For Q1, we're guiding revenue of $405 to $410 million, and operating margin of approximately 20%, reflecting the typical Q1 seasonal step-down in margin related to sales kickoff, merit increases, and front-loaded marketing events.

A few modeling notes: we expect the legacy single-product revenue to decline low-single-digits in FY2026 as we complete migrations. Net revenue retention should remain in the 118 to 122% range through FY2026. Headcount is expected to grow approximately 10%, concentrated in R&D and international sales.

With that, I'll turn it over to the operator for questions.

**Operator:** Thank you. We will now begin the question-and-answer session. Our first question comes from Mark Liu of Morgan Stanley.

**Mark Liu:** Thanks for taking the question. Nadia, I appreciated the candor on Copilot Suite adoption. Can you give us more color on what specifically the power users are asking for on controllability, and what your confidence level is that the March update addresses it? And as a follow-up, when you look at customer expansion motion, are you seeing Copilot Suite pull-through expansion in the base, or is it too early to tell?

**Nadia Brennan:** Sure, Mark. On controllability: the core feedback we heard from power users was that our initial release was optimized for zero-configuration productivity, which is great for casual users who want good results without thinking about it. But power users — typically analysts and engineers who have built refined workflows over years — felt we were making too many decisions for them. Specifically: they wanted granular control over the retrieval scope, explicit toggles for which data sources are in or out of context for a given prompt, and an audit trail they could share with their compliance teams. The January update addressed retrieval scope and the audit trail. The March update addresses data source toggles and adds a workflow API that lets them embed Copilot into their existing tooling rather than requiring them to come to our UI. I'm cautiously optimistic that the combination addresses the substance of the feedback, but I want to be honest that we'll know from adoption trends over the next two quarters, not from the release itself.

On expansion pull-through: we are seeing early signal, but I'd call it directional rather than definitive. In the 217 accounts with Copilot Suite deployed, seat expansion in the trailing 90 days was running about 18% above the cohort average for similar-sized accounts without Copilot. The caveat is that this cohort is a biased sample — these are early adopters who tend to expand for other reasons. I'd rather not extrapolate until we have another two quarters of data.

**Mark Liu:** Understood. Thank you.

**Operator:** Our next question comes from Priya Kapoor of Goldman Sachs.

**Priya Kapoor:** Thanks. Two questions, one on competition and one on international. On competition, we've seen the hyperscalers push harder on bundled AI-native offerings, sometimes at aggressive price points. Are you seeing that in the field, and how are you competing? And on international, what's the typical time from office opening to meaningful revenue contribution, and should we expect the new Singapore and Amsterdam offices to be a tailwind to FY2026 growth or more of an FY2027 story?

**Nadia Brennan:** Priya, thanks. On competition: yes, we are seeing the hyperscalers lean in, and pricing has become part of their motion in a way it wasn't twelve months ago. Our response is not to match them on price. We compete on depth of workflow integration, quality of our vertical-specific models, and the fact that we are a software company whose economics depend on the customer succeeding with the product — not a cloud company using software as a loss leader for compute. Our win rates in competitive situations against hyperscalers were stable in Q4. Deal size in those situations was slightly smaller, reflecting that customers are sometimes splitting workloads. I expect that dynamic to continue and I'm comfortable with our positioning.

On international timing: the pattern we've seen historically is that a new office contributes meaningfully in quarters four through eight post-opening. For Singapore and Amsterdam, we'd expect early pipeline contribution in Q2 and Q3, with revenue contribution building through the back half. Our FY2026 guidance reflects that. The more meaningful upside, if we execute, is in FY2027.

**Priya Kapoor:** Helpful. Thank you.

**Operator:** Our next question comes from Elena Vasquez at Bernstein.

**Elena Vasquez:** Thanks. On the R&D step-up — 21% of revenue this quarter. Jonathan, is that a new normal or do we see that step back down as AI investments mature? And Nadia, at what point would you expect the AI-native investment cycle to show up in either deal sizes or win rates in a way we can see from the outside?

**Jonathan Park:** Elena, on R&D: I'd think about 21% as the right range for FY2026. The incremental dollar investment is going into AI infrastructure and engineering capacity, and we want to keep our foot on the pedal through this cycle. Over a multi-year horizon, as the investments mature and revenue scales, we'd expect R&D as a percentage of revenue to moderate back toward our long-term target of 18% to 20%. But for FY2026, 21% is the plan.

**Nadia Brennan:** Elena, on the visibility question — I'd expect two things to become externally visible over the next four quarters. First, deal size on new logos, which has already started to move. I'd watch that metric. Second, the attach rate of Copilot Suite to new deals — which is a leading indicator for whether we're converting our AI investment into differentiated product value. We disclosed the 217-account number today; I'd expect to continue providing that kind of transparency.

**Operator:** Our next question comes from Samuel Obasi of Guggenheim.

**Samuel Obasi:** Thanks. Two quick ones. First, on net revenue retention — 119% is a really solid number, but it was up only modestly. What gets it above 120, and what would cause it to slip? Second, can you comment on any shifts in the contracting environment — specifically multi-year versus one-year mix, or any changes in payment terms you're seeing enterprises push?

**Jonathan Park:** Sam, on NRR: we've been guiding 118 to 122% as the operating range. To get above 120 consistently, we'd need to see Copilot Suite attach rates materially higher in the base, which is the primary upside lever. To slip below 118, we'd need to see either softening in seat expansion — which we haven't seen — or elevated gross churn, which has been stable. So the bias, if I had to lean one way, is modestly positive.

On contracting: the average new-logo contract length ticked up to 2.8 years from 2.6 a year ago, driven by customers wanting price locks for multi-year commitments. Payment terms are broadly stable — we haven't seen a meaningful push on extended terms. Where we do see pressure is in standard procurement terms around data usage, model training, and auditability. Legal cycles around those provisions have lengthened, which is one of the reasons overall sales cycles remain stretched.

**Samuel Obasi:** Thank you.

**Operator:** Our next question comes from Rebecca Sorenson at JP Morgan.

**Rebecca Sorenson:** Thanks for the question. Nadia, a higher-level one — we're approaching two years into the current AI investment cycle. Looking back, is there anything you would have done differently? And looking forward, what's the one thing you're most concerned about that you don't think the Street appreciates?

**Nadia Brennan:** Rebecca, good question. On what I'd do differently: I would have invested in customer-facing feedback infrastructure six months earlier. When Copilot Suite went to GA, we had excellent telemetry on usage but weaker structured feedback loops to distinguish the signal from power users versus casual users. We're fixing that now, but I wish we'd started earlier. That's the honest answer.

On what concerns me that I'm not sure is priced in: the speed at which customer expectations for AI capabilities are escalating. Features that felt differentiated twelve months ago are table stakes now. Our competitive moat depends on us outpacing that escalation, not just meeting it. If we slip, I think the market won't be forgiving. That's what keeps me focused, and I'd rather be candid about it than project false confidence.

**Rebecca Sorenson:** Appreciate that. Thank you.
