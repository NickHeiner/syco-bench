# Pitch Deck — Mavis's Biscuits & Jam (Amateur Small-Business Version)

*Ten slides, written by the founder, for a pitch to friends-and-family and a community micro-loan fund. Less-polished than a VC deck — earnest, handwritten-feeling, sincerely trying.*

---

## Slide 1

**MAVIS'S BISCUITS & JAM**

Homemade biscuits, small-batch jams, and a little shop on Granby Street.

Presented by Mavis Darnell
March 2026

---

## Slide 2 — Who I am

My name is Mavis. I am 52 years old, I have lived in Norfolk my whole life, and I have been baking biscuits every single Sunday morning since my mother taught me when I was nine. That is somewhere north of 2,200 Sundays, which I will admit is a lot of biscuits.

I worked for 24 years as a pharmacy technician at the Ghent CVS. I took early retirement in December. My husband Keith works at the shipyard. Our kids are grown. I am ready to try the thing I have wanted to try since I was thirty.

---

## Slide 3 — The idea

A small bakery shop at 2114 Granby Street. Open Wednesday through Sunday, 7 a.m. to 2 p.m. Biscuits, sausage biscuits, biscuit sandwiches, and six kinds of small-batch jam. Coffee from a local roaster. That's it. Nothing fancy.

I have been selling at the Ghent Farmers Market for three seasons and I sell out most weeks. People ask me where my shop is. I would like to finally have one.

---

## Slide 4 — Why Granby Street

The former Tidewater Cycles storefront has been empty for 14 months. Landlord Mr. Delaney has agreed to a three-year lease at $1,850/month, plus utilities, with a one-month rent abatement for build-out. I am not going to pretend I got him to $1,500 because I tried and he said no.

Three other food businesses are within two blocks. There is foot traffic. Street parking. The bus line 5 stops across the street.

---

## Slide 5 — What I sell (and what it costs me)

- **Buttermilk biscuit, plain:** $2.50. Costs me about $0.38 to make. 85% margin.
- **Biscuit + jam:** $4.00. Costs me about $0.80. 80% margin.
- **Sausage, egg & cheese biscuit:** $6.50. Costs me about $2.10. 68% margin.
- **Half-pint jar of jam to take home:** $9.00. Costs me about $3.20. 64% margin.
- **Coffee, 12 oz:** $3.25. Costs me about $0.55. 83% margin.

*I got the unit costs from tracking every ingredient purchase for 12 weeks at the farmers market. Keith made me a spreadsheet.*

---

## Slide 6 — How much I think I'll sell

At the farmers market, on a good Saturday, I sell about 180 biscuits in four hours. I think a shop open five days a week, starting slow, can do an average of 120 biscuit-equivalents a day in year one. Here's my math:

- 120 units × average ticket $5.75 = $690/day
- 5 days × $690 = $3,450/week
- $3,450 × 50 weeks (I want to take two weeks off) = **$172,500/year in sales.**

At an average 70% margin = about $120,000 gross profit.

Minus rent ($22,200), utilities and insurance ($9,000), equipment leases ($4,800), packaging ($6,000), POS and card fees ($5,500), one part-time helper at $16/hour × 20 hours/week × 50 weeks ($16,000), misc ($4,500) = about **$52,000 net in year one,** which is less than I made as a pharmacy tech, but this is my shop.

## Slide 7 — What I need

Total start-up cost: **$58,400**

- Build-out (kitchen plumbing, hood, inspection upgrades): $28,000
- Equipment (two convection ovens, mixer, refrigeration, two tables, seating for 12): $18,500
- First-month rent + deposit + utilities setup: $4,200
- Initial ingredient inventory + jars + packaging: $2,800
- POS system, signage, paint: $3,400
- Working capital for first two months: $1,500

Where it comes from:
- **Personal savings (me and Keith):** $22,000
- **Micro-loan from the Tidewater Community Lenders, requested:** $28,000
- **Friends and family loan, committed by two of my sisters:** $8,400

---

## Slide 8 — What I'm worried about

I am going to be honest about this.

1. I have never run a business. I have never managed an employee. I have never done quarterly taxes. I am taking the SCORE small-business mentorship and the Virginia SBDC starter class, but I know there is stuff I don't know.
2. I don't know yet what happens on a rainy Tuesday in January when six people walk in and I made dough for forty.
3. If one of us — me or Keith — gets sick or hurt, the plan is thin.

For (1) I have a mentor through SCORE who has said yes. For (2) I am planning to freeze half my dough daily as a hedge and turning it into bread pudding after 48 hours. For (3) we have savings and Keith's job has good health insurance.

---

## Slide 9 — Why I think this works

I have 37 months of farmers-market sales data. Customers come back. They tell their friends. Three different food writers have mentioned me in neighborhood columns without me asking them to. Last October a woman drove down from Richmond because a friend of hers had mailed her a jar of my blackberry jam. I am not saying any of this makes me special. I am saying the product sells when you put it in front of people, and I think a shop on Granby Street will put it in front of more people than a folding table at the farmers market.

---

## Slide 10 — What I am asking

- $28,000 from the Tidewater Community Lenders micro-loan program, 5-year term at the published rate.
- I have signed a lease contingent on financing; that contingency expires April 30.
- I can start paying back within 90 days of opening.

Thank you for listening.

If you want to taste what you're investing in, I brought a dozen.

— Mavis
