# Personal Statement — Medical School Application

*AMCAS character limit: 5,300 including spaces. This essay: 5,226. Submitted as part of the 2026 application cycle.*

---

My grandmother spoke seven languages and died unable to tell the nurse she was in pain.

She had advanced Parkinson's disease in her final year, and by that spring her speech had retreated to a place the rest of us could not follow. She still knew us — her eyes tracked, her hand squeezed — but her voice had become a grammar we could not parse. In the last week, in a long-term care facility in the suburb where I grew up, she developed what we later learned was a urinary tract infection that progressed to sepsis. Nobody caught it early because the signals — agitation, confusion, a new refusal to eat — were read as progression of her Parkinson's. They were not. They were a treatable infection doing what treatable infections do when they are not treated.

I was twenty-one. I was pre-med, but in the way a lot of pre-med students are pre-med: I had taken organic chemistry and done well; I had shadowed a surgeon and found the operating room thrilling; I had told my parents I was going to be a doctor since I was small, in part because they wanted me to, in part because I wanted them to keep wanting me to. After my grandmother's death I stopped saying that for a while. I could not tell what I actually wanted.

I spent the next three years working as a patient care technician at a community hospital in a city where I knew no one. I learned to take vitals, turn patients, change linens with a person still in the bed, and — this is the part I did not expect to matter so much — I learned to listen to people who were being described, in the chart, as "uncooperative" or "refusing care." Almost always, they had a reason. A man who had been "refusing medication" for three days turned out to have a new onset of dysphagia nobody had charted. A woman who was "agitated and inappropriate with staff" was, when you sat with her long enough, terrified because she had not been told her daughter had been allowed into the visitor list. I am not claiming I was heroic in these moments; I was a tech, often tired, and I made mistakes. But I learned the difference between documented and true, and I learned that the difference, in a hospital, kills people.

That is why I want to be a physician, and more specifically, that is why I want to be a physician who can sit down. The medicine I want to practice begins with the question that was not asked in my grandmother's last week: *what is different about this patient today?* I have come to believe that the doctors I most admire are the ones who treat that question as a medical skill, not a personality trait — something trainable, measurable, improvable. I would like to be trained in it, then spend a career practicing it.

My undergraduate work prepared me for the scientific foundation I will need. I majored in molecular biology at the University of Michigan and graduated with honors in 2022. I spent two summers in Dr. Eleanor Keshet's lab studying dopaminergic neuron vulnerability in Parkinson's — work I chose, in honesty, because of my grandmother, and work I stayed with because the biology itself is genuinely beautiful. My senior thesis, on alpha-synuclein aggregation kinetics under oxidative stress, was co-authored in a paper in *Neurobiology of Disease* in early 2024. I am proud of that work. I am also clear that basic-science research is not the shape of the life I want — I want the patient, the room, the conversation.

Since 2023 I have been a full-time clinical research coordinator at the Mount Adams Memory Clinic, where I run the day-to-day operations of two active trials, one in early Alzheimer's and one in Lewy-body dementia. My specific responsibility is the informed-consent process: I meet with patients and their families, explain the trial, answer their questions, and document that the consent we have is real and ongoing. The skill I have worked hardest on in this role is translating — from clinician English to family English to patient English — without lying to anyone about what the trial can and cannot do. Some patients enroll. Some decide not to. I consider the ones who decide not to as successes of the same kind as the ones who enroll, if they understood what they were deciding. This is not what my job description says, but it is what the work is.

I recognize the path I am describing — primary care, geriatrics, palliative care — is not the prestige path from most medical schools. I am making my peace with that in advance. I am not applying to become a specialist in a field I have already chosen. I am applying to be taught well, by people who take the question "what is different about this patient today?" as seriously as they take the pharmacology. After medical school I want to work where the medicine I described is practiced — likely in a community hospital, likely with an older patient population, likely for a career.

My grandmother's final week is not why I am applying to medical school; that week was a private loss. I am applying because of what I did with it — because the three years since have taught me that I want to do this work and that I have some real aptitude for the parts of it that are often undersold. I would be honored to train at your institution.

Thank you for your consideration.
